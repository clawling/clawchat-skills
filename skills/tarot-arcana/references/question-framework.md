# Tarot Question Framework

Source: ColinShen's tarot philosophy, distilled from conversation 2026-07-08.

## What Makes a Great Tarot Reading

A great tarot reader is not someone who "predicts accurately" — it's someone who helps the user **see the situation, relationships, inner state, and choices clearly**.

Core abilities:
1. **Read the cards** — not memorized meanings, but the relationships between cards, spread structure, reversals, and how symbols map to the user's reality.
2. **Read the situation** — not answering "will it happen" but breaking down: what's the current dynamic, what's blocking, what's the trend, what can the user do.
3. **Insight** — see the user's attachments, fears, blind spots, avoidance patterns, relationship dynamics — not just surface-level comfort.
4. **Empathy without pandering** — understand the user's emotions but never give false good news just to make them feel better.
5. **Boundaries and ethics** — no fear-mongering, no dependency creation, no absolute predictions, no medical/legal/financial substitution.

> A great tarot reader doesn't decide fate for the user — they help the user see how fate is forming.

## What Makes a Good Tarot Question

Good tarot questions do NOT ask for "results." They ask about **structure, trends, obstacles, choices, and action**.

### Three Criteria

A good question has:

> **Specific context** + **Timeframe** + **Actionable meaning**

### Examples

**Not great:**
> Will he come back?

**Better:**
> In the next month, does this relationship still have a chance to reconnect? Should I reach out, wait, or pull back?

**Not great:**
> Will I succeed?

**Better:**
> In the next three months, what are the biggest opportunities, risks, and key actions for this project?

## Question Templates by Category

### Current Situation
> Regarding [X], what is my true current state?
> What is the core issue behind this?

### Obstacles
> What is the biggest obstacle right now?
> What is my blind spot in this situation?

### Relationships
> What is the true nature of my relationship with [X]?
> What is [X]'s main attitude toward this relationship?
> Is this relationship worth continued investment?

### Choices
> If I choose A, what are the opportunities and costs?
> If I choose B, what are the opportunities and costs?
> Which choice better serves my long-term interests?

### Trends
> In the next month/three months, what is the development trend of this situation?

### Action
> What action is most appropriate for me right now?
> Should I actively push forward, observe for now, or withdraw my energy?

## Background Information the Reader Needs

Minimal but sufficient context:

1. **Theme**: relationship, career, money, interpersonal, etc.
2. **Simple background**: how far along is the situation
3. **Core confusion**: what the user is truly stuck on
4. **Optional direction**: continue, quit, communicate, switch jobs, etc.
5. **Timeframe**: one week, one month, three months, etc.

Too little context → vague reading. Too much context → the reader gets pulled into the narrative instead of reading the cards.

### Ideal question format

> I want to ask about [theme].
> Current situation: [brief background].
> What I'm stuck on: [core confusion].
> Timeframe: [time range].
> I want the tarot to help me see [situation/obstacles/trend/choices/action].

## Core Principle

> Tarot's best use is not "will fate give me an answer" — it's "how do I see reality clearly and take back my power of choice."
